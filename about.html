Comparison of Window / Shading Performance

Data & Visualizations of EnergyPlus Simulations

Background

In 1997 ODL developed the first blinds between insulated glass panels (BBGs), which were installed primarily in residential entry doors. In 2016 ODL launched between glass blinds for windows, which are now sold to a range of large and small window OEMs. These blinds are manually controlled today. Historically energy performance has been characterized by U-factor and SHGC metrics. With the establishment of the Attachments Energy Rating Council (AERC) the DOE has supported studies assessing the whole building energy savings of attached window coverings, specifically cellular shades and horizontal blinds. These studies do not account for blind slat angle control which allow for direct or diffuse solar radiation while providing natural daylight. There are no comparable studies for BBGs and other forms of dynamic glazing. The DOE also sponsored studies of the effectiveness of different window covering control strategies, anticipating the use of automated window coverings in smart homes.

In 2019 the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued the first ENERGY STAR® Smart Home Energy Management System (SHEMS) specification. AERC certified automated window coverings are listed as an “encouraged” optional device. There was pushback against including ENERGY STAR windows with dynamic glazing, specifically BBGs, based on the belief that the ENERGY STAR windows rating does not address dynamic glass and that there is no connected criteria for windows.

Objective of this Dashboard

Simulations were done in EnergyPlus (v 9.2) using the National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) prototype residential building that was modified by the AERC. The locations included a cold climate (Minneapolis, MN) and a hot climate (Houston, TX).

Simulations were done for unshaded windows, windows with BBGs and Venetian blinds with slat angles between -75 and 75 degrees at increments of 15 degrees, and windows with 3 types of cellular blinds. The insulating class units (IGUs) where specific for each climate, including window systems that meet the ENERGY STAR requirements for that location.

The EnergyPlus simulation results were compiled in using Python/Pandas and then formatted for export. Exported data was used in Tableau to create visualizations specific for this project. The visualizations are intended to look at both the energy impacts of shading devices and the impacts of natural daylighting. Daylighting and illumnance data was recorded at a reference point on the south elevation, 36 inches from the exterior wall and 30 inches from the floor. The visualizations are intended to display the trade-offs on the insulating values of shading devices compared the availability of natural light.
